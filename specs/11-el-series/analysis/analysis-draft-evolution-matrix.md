## II. OPENING SEQUENCE EVOLUTION

### D1: Naturalistic Domestic ("A Girl and Her Dogs")
- El slouches on couch; Dave/Sandra discuss job loss and finances
- **No surrealist elements whatsoever** - pure domestic realism
- Job list discussion leads to dog-walking idea
- Sheen introduced as ~20-year-old artist neighbor; visibly snorts Percocet (p.19)
- El uses notepad extensively for communication
- Barb (Hugh's wife) is alive; Benji is their dog
- Grounded in recognizable reality throughout

### D4: Surrealist Intrusion
- Vines "crawl out from underneath the darkness of the couch" from page one
- Surrealist escalation during job discussion:
  - Sandra's briefcase "swallows her entire torso"
  - Papers "swarm around their heads" like "Kamikaze pilots"
  - Vines "force their way into her mouth" and "crawl down her throat, choking her"
- **Dog barking causes vines to retreat** (dogs as salvation motif)
- Frank introduced early: stalks El in yellow car, **SHOOTS** at her near school
- Reality already unstable from page one

### D7: Dissociative/Pharmaceutical Frame
```
BLACK. Pill bottle RATTLES.
FADE IN: a translucent BODY drops a glowing yellow orb into
the top of its viscous head. The orb spirals down, dissolving
in The Body's gut then branching through The Body's veins.
```
- **NEW**: "The Body" as dissociative visual metaphor
- Medication/pharmaceutical context explicit
- El's interior state externalized before she appears
- Vines THEN emerge (layered surrealism)

**Dramaturgical Implication**: D7 establishes El's psychological fragmentation from the first frame, priming the audience for later fantasy sequences as internal states rather than objective reality.

## III. "NO" AS RECURRING MOTIF

The word "No" functions as the screenplay's thematic spine, evolving across drafts:

### Instances in D7:
1. **Hugh's testimony** (p.80): "She has always said yes before without any lip. Just like that she declines me, flips like a light switch."
2. **Court revelation** (p.80): Hugh: "'No.'" / Prosecutor Dara: "This is the type of person who thinks she can say no."
3. **El's refusal to testify** (pp.85-86): Repeatedly answers "No." to Judge Dara
4. **FINAL LINE** (p.87): Sandra demands Albie be gone. El "stands tall... She smiles. *No.*"

**Arc**: "No" transforms from pathologized silence/refusal (what's "wrong" with El) to empowered assertion of boundaries. The very thing that makes her "difficult" becomes her liberation.

## IV. FANTASY SEQUENCE EXPANSION

### D1-D4: Fantasy Contained
- Art room "paradise door" exists but limited
- Fantasy sequences shorter, more dreamlike
- Clearer reality/fantasy demarcation

### D7: Extended Alternate Realities

#### A. Julia's Chateau Sequence (pp.63-72)
- **Setting**: Late-1800s Eastern European Chateau with moat, KGB Officers, alligator-filled swamps
- **Julia** (20s): Appears with halo that fades to crown of thorns
- **The Apostles**: Barely-clad women at dinner table
- **Drugging**: Foul-tasting water, El's euphoria
- **Surveillance Reveal**: Screens showing El's home from every angle
- **Violence**: Alek carries El up spiral staircase; she bites his hand, escapes
- **Momma-Gator**: 20-foot alligator blocking road; Alek shoots

**Dramaturgical Function**: Represents El's internalized understanding of trafficking operations as a religious cult/compound—the "chateau" as both literal threat and metaphor for systems that capture vulnerable girls.

#### B. Surreal Courtroom Sequence (pp.75-86)
- **Everyone named "Dara"**: Judge Dara, Prosecutor Dara, Officer Dara, Court Stenographer Dara
- **Absurdist charges**: "libel, disturbing a crime scene, not respecting the elders or the law, not going to church"
- **Witnesses**: Hugh (rambling, reveals adoption), Jeannie (holy woman), The Reporter (protects sources)
- **El's defense**: Hands jury card reading "it wasn't me"

**Dramaturgical Function**: Externalizes El's experience of a hostile system where everyone in power is interchangeable ("Dara") and the charges are impossible to contest. The trial isn't about truth—it's about conformity.

## VI. COMMUNICATION METHODS

El's selective mutism is handled through multiple modes:

1. **Written cards** (D7 refined): El hands jury card reading "it wasn't me"; jury passes it down the line
2. **Texting** (consistent across drafts): Primary communication with peers
3. **Physical gesture**: Shaking head "No!", standing tall at end
4. **Notepad** (earlier drafts, reduced in D7)
5. **Phone** (for dog-walking business communication)

**Evolution**: D7 trusts visual communication more—El's final "No" is embodied, not written.

## IX. ENDING COMPARISON

### All Drafts: El says "No" to Sandra

**D7 Refinement**:
```
SANDRA
He (pointing to Albie) needs to be gone now.

Dave puts his hand on El's shoulder, sighs, and just as he
turns to say--

El stops him, urging him aside. She stands tall...

She smiles.

No.
```

The ending crystallizes across drafts, but D7's version emphasizes:
1. Dave's potential complicity (about to comply)
2. El's physical assertion (stopping Dave, standing tall)
3. The smile before "No"—not just refusal, but ownership
4. Albie (the dog) as contested ground—what El refuses to surrender

## X. FEEDBACK CORRELATION

### Nicholl Reader Comments (September 2019)

**Reader 1:**
- Praised: "vibrant character," "real visual sense," "vines grabbing onto her legs" as symbolic
- Dog-walking provides narrative spine; Sheen friendship "mined for some good moments"
- **Criticisms:**
  - "Rather plot-light overall" with "no particular stakes"
  - "El's random encounters with various people...doesn't really get much shaping"
  - After Sheen's death: "script feels like it loses its way a bit, getting increasingly surreal late"
  - "Hard to know what to make of an action sequence involving female school bullies and a gun"
  - "Climactic courtroom sequence that also seems to be completely imaginary"
  - "Ends without really resolving anything"
  - Needs "more shape and consistency"

**Reader 2:**
- Voice "imaginative and confident"
- "Couldn't tell what was supposed to be real and what was supposed to be created in El's brain"
- "Script never gave clues with which the reader could distinguish fact from fantasy"
- Questions if "fantasy shedded light on the reason for her muteness"
- Notes El "found her voice" but since "No" is "not written as dialogue, perhaps she just thinks this"
- "Didn't understand the family dynamic. Did El not speak because of their constant bickering? Was she adopted?"
- Found "magic in some of the visuals, such as the vines"

### D7 Responses to Feedback

| Feedback Concern | D7 Response |
|------------------|-------------|
| Fantasy/reality confusion | "The Body" opening establishes subjective frame; fantasy sequences are clearly El's psychological space |
| Silent protagonist illegibility | Card system refined; physical acting emphasized; "No" as spoken climax |
| Pacing in Act 2 | Chateau and courtroom sequences provide escalating set-pieces |
| Stakes clarity | Trafficking operation fully visualized; surveillance reveal raises stakes |
| Family backstory unclear | Hugh's testimony explicitly reveals adoption (p.80) |

The prose treatment (August 2018) reveals a transitional conception between D4 and D7:

### Julian → Julia Transformation
| Treatment (Aug 2018) | D7 (Aug 2019) |
|---------------------|---------------|
| **Julian** (34): Male Russian landlord brother | **Julia** (20s): Female chateau leader with halo/thorns |

| Treatment | D7 |
|-----------|-----|
| Courtroom occurs IN El's apartment | Abstract surreal courtroom space |
| "Everyone" files into living room | Everyone named "Dara" (homogenized authority) |
| Characters retain individual names | Individual identities collapsed into system |
| Trial structure more realistic | Absurdist charges ("not going to church") |

### "No" Delivery Trajectory
| Version | Delivery |
|---------|----------|
| Treatment | "with her voice she says, 'no.'" (explicit speech) |
| D4 | "She mouths-- *No*" (ambiguous) |
| D7 | "She stands tall... She smiles. *No*." (embodied assertion) |

**Dramaturgical Implication**: The treatment conceived El's "No" as unambiguously spoken, but D4 introduced delivery ambiguity that Nicholl readers flagged. D7 attempts to resolve this through physicalized assertion (standing tall, smiling) while maintaining the italic styling.

## XIII. POST-NICHOLL REVISION (Jan 2020)

The "first scene" revision (January 2020, labeled "2019 Q4 Seventh Draft") shows active development responding to competition feedback:

### Opening Sequence Expansion
- **"The Body" metaphor retained and expanded**: Full bathroom sequence with The Body showering, dressing, checking phone before transition to El
- **New grounding details**: 80s-style bathroom decor, morning routine, smartphone notification
- **El confirmed at 16**: Age change from D1 (14) maintained

### Family Dynamic Clarification
- **Racial difference explicit**: "Both are considerably fairer in skin than El" - adoption subtext now visual
- **Private school stakes**: "We're taking you out of private school" adds economic pressure
- **"Condition" vs "problem" dialogue**: Dave/Sandra debate El's muteness terminology, addressing Nicholl concern about unclear family dynamic

### Surrealism Maintained
Despite feedback about reality/fantasy confusion, the revision retains signature elements:
- Vines "crawl out from underneath the darkness of the couch"
- Briefcase "nearly swallows her entire torso"
- Papers "swarm around their heads, little Kamikaze pilots dive to draw blood"

**Implication**: The author interpreted Nicholl feedback not as requiring removal of surrealist imagery but as needing better contextual grounding. The opening's domestic detail (bathroom, private school discussion, racial difference notation) provides reality anchors while preserving the fantastical visual language.

## XIV. THEMATIC SYNTHESIS

**D1**: A mute girl navigates hostile environments; emphasis on isolation
**D4**: Surrealist elements externalize internal states; trafficking emerges as systemic threat
**D7**: Full integration of psychological, social, and criminal dimensions; El's "No" becomes act of resistance against all systems that demand her compliance
**2020 Revision**: Grounding details added while maintaining surrealist vocabulary; family dynamics clarified

The screenplay evolves from **individual trauma narrative** to **systemic critique**—the "Daras" of the courtroom, the surveillance apparatus, the chateau compound all represent interlocking systems that target vulnerable girls. El's silence is reframed: not disability but refusal to participate in her own exploitation.
